# Horde Magic 2.0: Introducing The Raid

Source: <https://www.quietspeculation.com/2013/02/horde-magic-2-0-introducing-the-raid/>

Author: Peter Knudson  
Published: February 18, 2013

This article revisits Horde Magic and proposes **Raid**, a staged variant where the Horde deck becomes more like a roleplaying quest or dungeon.

## Premise

The article frames Horde Magic as a story generator. A good Horde deck should not just be a pile of enemy cards; it should explain what the Survivors are fighting, why the battle matters, and what kind of memorable moments the table is trying to create.

Raid pushes this further by letting the Horde designer act a bit like a dungeon master.

## Raid Rules

Raid uses normal Horde Magic as its base, with three major changes:

1. The Horde deck is semi-ordered before play.
2. Cards inside each quest or stage are randomized, but the stages occur in a set order.
3. Artifacts revealed by the Horde do not count as non-token cards; they become loot for the Survivors.
4. Survivors cannot attack the Horde deck directly.

Because Survivors cannot mill the Horde by attacking, Raid is more defensive and scenario-driven.

## Why Semi-Order The Deck?

The deck order lets the scenario tell a story. Survivors can fight through one stage, then another, then a boss or finale.

Within each stage, the cards can still be randomized so the game does not play out exactly the same way every time.

## Example: The Dragonborn

The article's first Raid example is inspired by Skyrim. The Survivors fight through wilderness creatures, soldiers, vampires, undead, dragons, and a final boss.

### Stage Order

1. Green cards and tokens.
2. First Dragon Boss.
3. White cards and tokens.
4. Second Dragon Boss.
5. Black cards and tokens.
6. Red cards and Zombie tokens.
7. Final Boss.

### Story Shape

The Survivors are trapped in hostile wilderness, then move through escalating conflicts before confronting a world-ending dragon threat.

The author notes that this list may favor the Survivors and suggests possible fixes such as adding a reset rule after each stage or adding more sweepers.

## Example: Rat Horde

The second example uses Gatecrash-era Rat support.

The setup is a city overrun by rats and a criminal syndicate. Survivors must exterminate the infestation before disease overtakes the city.

### Rat Horde Design Notes

- Rat tokens are small, so the deck uses anthem-like effects to scale over time.
- Poison counters act as a doom clock.
- If ten poison counters are revealed, the Survivors lose.
- Sorin emblems are used like token flips: they continue the reveal chain and permanently buff the rats.
- Bad Moon appears as an additional scaling hedge.

## Takeaways For Game-Night Boxes

Raid is especially relevant to boxed game nights because it supports scenario packaging:

- A staged enemy deck.
- Boss cards or boss packets.
- Loot cards.
- Scenario sheets.
- Difficulty adjustments between stages.
- A campaign structure where the table fights multiple Raids in sequence.

Raid also makes Horde more self-contained. If the box includes Survivor decks, the host can tune both sides into a fixed cooperative experience.
