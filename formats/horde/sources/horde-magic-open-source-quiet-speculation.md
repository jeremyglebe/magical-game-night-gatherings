# Horde Magic: An Open Source Project

Source: <https://www.quietspeculation.com/2011/10/horde-magic-an-open-source-project/>

Author: Peter Knudson  
Published: October 26, 2011

This follow-up article treats Horde Magic as an open-source casual format. It gathers early community feedback, discusses scaling and planeswalkers, introduces preconstructed Survivor decks, shares alternate Horde deck ideas, and proposes a first banned list.

## Main Design Point

The article argues that the format should be easy to change. If the Zombie Horde is too easy or too hard, the first lever should usually be the Horde deck itself rather than making the rules more complicated.

## Scaling Problems

Some players found the Horde too easy with more Survivors. The article suggests reducing setup turns for 3-4 player games from three turns to two.

The author prefers preserving the simple rules engine and increasing difficulty through card selection:

- Remove weak Horde cards.
- Add more threatening non-token cards.
- Include better sweepers or reset effects.
- Tune the Horde deck to the Survivor decks being used.

| Card | Reason |
| --- | --- |

| All Is Dust | Broad reset effect that punishes overbuilt defenses. |

| Vulturous Zombie | Adds a large flying threat. |
| Living Death | Flavorful, swingy board reset. |

## Planeswalker Rule

The original article did not handle planeswalkers cleanly.

The proposed solution:

1. If Survivors control one or more planeswalkers, flip a coin at the beginning of Horde combat.
2. On heads, the Horde randomly allocates attackers to planeswalkers.
3. Allocate one Zombie per point of loyalty for each planeswalker, starting in random planeswalker order if there are multiple.

This is not elegant, but it gives planeswalkers risk without making the Horde too decision-heavy.

## Preconstructed Survivor Decks

The article suggests building fixed Survivor Commander decks designed to fight the Horde. These could work like party roles: Fighter, Cleric, Wizard, and so on.

The included example is a green-white Survivor deck by `@mtgcolorpie`, with **Tolsimir Wolfblood** as commander. The article likes it because it is thematic, not overpowered, and full of defensive / soldier-flavored cards.

## Alternate Horde Deck Ideas

The article collects several community Horde concepts.

### Sliver Horde

Credit: `@wobbles`

The Sliver Horde uses small Slivers that become more dangerous as lords accumulate. The article notes that Sliver Overlord could be placed at the bottom of the deck to create an end-boss feeling.

### Cat Horde

Credit: `d0su` from MTG Salvation

Cat tokens are close to Zombie tokens in baseline stats, which makes them easier to adapt. The deck mixes mediocre cats, larger threats, and multicolor finishers.

### Merfolk Horde

Credit: `@Bassiuz`

The Merfolk Horde uses smaller tokens but leans on lords and disruptive blue-white effects. The article calls out cards such as Hysterical Blindness, Summon the School, and Balance as examples of collection finds that can become Horde cards.

## Takeaways For Game-Night Boxes

This article is especially useful for boxed-set design because it opens the door to:

- Tuning difficulty by changing the Horde deck.
- Building class-like Survivor decks.
- Creating themed Horde boxes beyond Zombies.
- Maintaining a matchup-specific ban/watch list.
- Treating Horde Magic as a flexible engine rather than one fixed ruleset.
